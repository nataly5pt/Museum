Mask of the Harmattan — Exhibit Label

Facts

Material: Carved iroko wood; fiber fringe; mineral pigment.

Approx. date: 17th–19th c. (oral chronologies).

Region: Inland Sahel trade corridor.

Dimensions: 36 × 22 × 14 cm. On display courtesy of the Diallo family archive.

Interpretation

The mask is associated with the season when the dry Harmattan wind arrives, blanching sky and skin alike. Its slit eyes and jutting jaw exaggerate scarcity: breath is short, water is counted, words are measured. During village processions that mark the first dust-laden gusts, the wearer moves in staccato bursts, stopping to “drink” air through the maw while the chorus rattles gourds—an auditory stand-in for sand against a calabash. The pigments are not merely decorative: ochre and kaolin are rubbed in with oil at the end of each season, sealing prayers for endurance into the wood. While outsiders often read such masks as fixed “spirits,” the custodians insist the figure is relational: it becomes a person only when dust, song, dancer, and witness meet.

Comparative Notes

Similar dust-season effigies appear from Senegambia to Hausaland. Some emphasize tooth rows; others, filament beards that “sweep” paths clear. Compared with coastal masquerades, Harmattan figures tend to minimize ornament and maximize silhouette—perhaps because everything extra is lost to the wind.

Cautionary Note on Interpretation

Dates and functions are based on overlapping oral accounts gathered a century apart. Without a secure excavation context, any single narrative would be misleading; this label preserves plurality where certainty is not possible.

On display, courtesy of: The Diallo family archive.

Basaltic Glyphstone — Collection Note

Material: Basalt, iron-rich patina.

Function: Boundary/rite marker; possible oath stone.

Location Found: Inland Niger Delta terrace, near an abandoned causeway.

Interpretation

The shallow incisions on the stone do not form an alphabet; they index presence. A keeper would score the surface during oath-making, tracing lines that paired a name with a promise. The stone’s working face slopes to shed rain, and the upper edge is smoothed by repeated touch. Microscopic striations suggest reed pens charged with ochre were occasionally drawn through the grooves, brightening cuts so they could be “counted” in morning light. Basalt was not quarried locally; the boulder was dragged from an older lava field and recentered here, which confers gravity— a sense that law is anchored to deep time rather than a single reign. The criss-crossing scars read like a net: each pledge catches on another, holding the community together even as generations pass.

Riverbank Water Jar — Collection Note

Material: Low-fired clay with vegetal temper; soot accretions.

Function: Storage and cooling of potable water.

Location Found: Collapsed riverside shelter, silted to the rim.

Interpretation

This jar bears heat in reverse. Firing was brief and cool, leaving the walls porous so water could breathe out and evaporate, drawing away warmth. Soot on the shoulder indicates proximity to a hearth, yet the interior is lime-clean, suggesting a customary wash of ash-water to sweeten taste. A thumb-pressed cordon around the neck is both decoration and grip; its rhythm matches counting songs used to time chores at dawn. Pots like this are rarely signed; the potter’s identity is carried by clay source and coil cadence. Found upright amid flood silt, the vessel likely floated as the shelter broke, then settled as the water retreated—a quiet survival that mirrors its function: to keep calm, cool life at the center of a hot day.

Copper Trade Ring — Collection Note

Material: Copper alloy with malachite patina.

Function: Wearable currency / bridewealth token.

Location Found: Market mound, edge of caravan staging ground.

Interpretation

Too large for a finger, too heavy for a toe, this ring lived on the wrist or hung from a trade belt where its soft chime announced solvency. Copper carried stories: from ore hills to smelting hearths, from smithy to stall. The ring’s flat inner face reduces skin pinch; the outer bevel catches light, a small sun advertising value even when dust dulls the surface. Copper’s green is not neglect but memory—minerals returning toward earth. Oral accounts pair such rings with negotiations that balanced desire and obligation: what is a fair exchange when futures are also being traded? The ring’s worn notch implies it was tied as collateral more than once. Currency, yes; but also a promise looped and reopened as lives intertwined.

Mask of the Harmattan — Collection Note

Material: Iroko wood; fiber fringe; mineral pigment.

Function: Seasonal masquerade; wind-season rites.

Location Found: Custodial house near Sahel caravan node.

Interpretation

The mask “arrives” with the wind, not with a person. Custodians say it sleeps on a shelf until the first dust finds it; only then may a dancer carry the face into the square. Slit eyes control air to the wearer, forcing short breaths that match the drum’s clipped meter. That constraint is moral: during scarcity, take in little, give out song. Pigments were renewed annually with oil that smelled of peanuts and prayer. While museums read faces as individuals, the community speaks of weather kin—figures that belong to a family of forces. Its power is thus not ownership but right relation: honoring what arrives and always leaves again.

Leather Talisman Pouch — Collection Note

Material: Tanned goat leather; sinew; vegetal dye; paper amulet.

Function: Personal protection; portable blessing.

Location Found: Family trunk with travel gear and prayer beads.

Interpretation

The pouch is a house for small words. Inside, a folded square bears a verse written by an itinerant scholar who exchanged ink for millet on his way between river towns. The leather is dyed with acacia pods; the color darkens around the flap where fingers worried it before sleep. Triangular points along the seam reference teeth—signs that the pouch “bites” misfortune before it can enter a life. Unlike luxury cases, this one shows repairs that honor use: a mended stitch in bright thread turns damage into witness. To wear it was to carry conversation: between family, teacher, and the One who listens through dust and rain alike.

Cowrie-Adorned Belt — Collection Note

Material: Plaited plant fiber; cowrie shells; indigo thread.

Function: Ceremonial dress; status marker; rhythm accent in dance.

Location Found: Heirloom chest from a river-port household.

Interpretation

Cowries once priced salt, cloth, and companionship. Sewn to a belt, they announce both wealth and belonging: each shell’s mouth clacks softly against its neighbor to keep time with steps. The plait shows a five-over pattern common in river towns, and the indigo knots were added later to tighten a stretch—evidence that garments live as bodies change. Some cowries are older than the belt itself, drilled on a different axis; they are stories adopted into a new home. In performance, light catches the porcelain curves like river sparks at dusk, reminding the crowd that all journeys—water, trade, marriage—circle back through the same bright channel.
